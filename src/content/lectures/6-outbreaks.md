## 2. Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Outbreak** | (a) Two or more people with similar illness linked in time or place; (b) Greater than expected rate vs usual background rate; (c) A **single case** for rare diseases (diphtheria, botulism, rabies, VHF, polio) |
| **Epidemic** | Sudden increase in cases above what is normally expected in that population/area |
| **Pandemic** | Epidemic occurring worldwide, crossing international boundaries, affecting large numbers simultaneously |

> "Outbreak" and "epidemic" carry the same definition, but "outbreak" is often used for a more limited geographical area

## 5. Outbreak Control Teams

- Following outbreak declaration, decision made on urgency to form **Outbreak Control Team (OCT)**
- In UK hospital setting, OCT comprises:
  - **Consultant Microbiologist**
  - Medical Clinician(s)
  - **Infection Control Nurse(s)**
- Primary objective: **protect public health** by identifying source and implementing control measures to prevent further spread/recurrence

### 5.1 Steps in Outbreak Investigation

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1. **Confirm the outbreak** | Verify diagnosis; compare current rate with baseline (expected) rate |
| 2. **Define a case** | Develop **case definition** (clinical criteria + person/place/time) |
| 3. **Find cases** | Active surveillance; contact tracing |
| 4. **Descriptive epidemiology** | Characterise by **person** (age, sex, occupation), **place** (mapping), **time** (epidemic curve) |
| 5. **Generate hypothesis** | Source/vehicle/mode of transmission |
| 6. **Test hypothesis** | Analytical studies (case-control, cohort); statistical testing |
| 7. **Implement control measures** | Isolation, treatment, environmental decontamination, vaccination |
| 8. **Communicate findings** | Report to UKHSA, media if needed, scientific publication |

### 5.2 Epidemic Curves

| Shape | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **Point source** | Sharp peak then decline; single exposure event (e.g. wedding food poisoning) |
| **Continuous source** | Prolonged plateau; ongoing exposure (e.g. contaminated water supply) |
| **Propagated** | Successive peaks; person-to-person transmission (e.g. norovirus ward outbreak) |

## 6. Outbreak Data Collection

### 6.1 Patient Information

- Age, location, predisposing conditions, **travel history**
- Information collected determines which diagnostic tests to perform
- **Medical history is critical**

### 6.2 NHS Monitoring

- NHS England regional teams assess UKHSA data to predict surge in service demand
- Surveillance from PHE/UKHSA helps predict outbreaks of **influenza, norovirus**, and other seasonal illnesses
- Data collected daily from **GP practices, 111 services, out-of-hours GPs, A&E departments**
- Enables hospitals to plan and avoid disruption

## 7. Surveillance Systems

### 7.1 Global Level

| Organisation | Scope |
|---|---|
| **WHO** | Global surveillance and coordination |
| **CDC** (US) | National and international |
| **ECDC** (European) | EU-wide disease surveillance |

> ECDC example: Measles cases in EU **trebled in 2017**. Highest: Romania (10,623), Italy (4,991), Greece (1,463), Germany (926)

### 7.2 UK National Level

- **UKHSA** (Colindale, North London) -- formerly CDSC
- Derives surveillance data from **clinical microbiology laboratories** (England and Wales)
- Based on **laboratory-confirmed cases** (pathogen isolated from patient/food/water/environmental sample)
- **Mandatory surveillance** requires clinical labs to forward data on specific microbes
- Published as **Health Protection Reports** (monthly/quarterly)

### 7.3 Surveillance Dashboards

- John Hopkins University COVID dashboard (historical)
- ECDC/WHO live dashboards for epidemics/pandemics

## 8. UK Public Health Structure

## 9. Viral Outbreaks

### 9.1 SARS-CoV (2003)

- First severe, readily transmissible new disease of 21st century
- First reported SE Asia 2003; global outbreak
- >8,000 affected worldwide; **774 deaths**
- Requires **CL3 laboratory**
- UK: only 4 cases

### 9.2 Swine Flu H1N1 (2009-2010)

- First pandemic with specific **vaccine available** while virus still causing disease
- Surveillance: GP symptom reporting + lab-confirmed cases + online patient algorithm
- **"Buddy" system**: infected person given number code; buddy collects antiviral from designated provider

### 9.3 MERS-CoV (2012)

- Novel coronavirus; first appeared **Saudi Arabia ~2012**
- **~35% mortality**
- Most infections human-to-human; **camels** = major reservoir host
- Healthcare-associated outbreaks in Saudi Arabia, UAE, Republic of Korea

### 9.4 SARS-CoV-2 / COVID-19 (2019-2023)

- Novel coronavirus; first identified **Wuhan, China, December 2019**
- Declared pandemic by WHO **11 March 2020**
- Transmission: **respiratory droplets and aerosols**; also fomite
- UK response: lockdowns, test-and-trace, vaccination programme
- **Diagnostics**: RT-PCR (gold standard); lateral flow device (LFD) rapid antigen test
- **Vaccination**: Multiple platforms -- mRNA (Pfizer/BioNTech, Moderna), viral vector (AstraZeneca/Oxford), protein subunit
- Led to creation of **UKHSA** (Oct 2021) replacing PHE
- COVID Inquiry: scrutinised UKHSA leadership decisions including discharge of COVID-positive patients to care homes

### 9.5 Zika Virus (2016)

- Transmitted by mosquito vector (*Aedes*)
- May pass during pregnancy to foetus -- **severe birth defects**

## 10. Bacterial Outbreaks

### 10.1 Cholera -- Yemen

- 18.8 million affected by war
- Cholera outbreak began **2016** -- **largest ever recorded** (>1 million cases)
- Caused by *Vibrio cholerae*

### 10.2 Diphtheria -- Yemen

- Since October 2017: >300 cases
- Caused by *Corynebacterium diphtheriae*
- Transmitted by close physical/respiratory contact
- **Fatal in 5-10% of respiratory diphtheria cases**
- Response: Diphtheria Task Force (MoPHP + WHO + UNICEF) -- antibiotics, antitoxin, immunisation

### 10.3 Measles -- Europe

- Outbreaks in France, Bulgaria, Italy, Poland, Romania
- ECDC surveillance: https://www.ecdc.europa.eu/en/measles

### 10.4 Tuberculosis -- Leicester

- **Largest ever TB outbreak in UK** (2001): >300 cases
- Crown Hills Community College
- Failure to use preventive TB therapy may have been a contributing factor
- PHE data (2000-2016): Proportion of UK-born TB cases by ethnic minority tracked

## 11. Avian Influenza

### 11.1 HPAI H5N1

- Confirmed in commercial poultry (Nov 2022)
- **3 km protection zone** with culling of birds
- Currently **NOT human-to-human** transmission
- **Hazard**: when bird-to-human strain becomes human-to-human strain

### 11.2 Bernard Matthews Outbreak (2007)

- Avian influenza in turkeys at Holton, Suffolk
- **152,000 turkeys culled** (7,000 infected)
- EUR 800K compensation
- All contacts offered health screening
- 2017: Avian influenza again at Bernard Matthews (Frith)

### 11.3 Influenza Vaccines

- **Influenza A, B, and C** -- annual vaccine composition updated
- Spanish Flu epidemic (1920s) -- no vaccine, no treatment available

## 12. Surveillance in LEDCs

- **Less robust** due to lack of funding and limited sensitivity/specificity of diagnostic methods
- Example: Tamale University Hospital, Ghana -- HIV, Hepatitis, Malaria, AST, and TB testing
- Surveillance limited to laboratory testing undertaken
- **Improving surveillance**: Modify diagnostic methods (e.g. **auramine staining** for TB using fluorescence microscopy)
  - Why important? TB is fatal if untreated, rapidly transmissible, treatment is expensive, increasing MDR/XDR-TB
  - Manchester Hospital donated fluorescence microscope to improve detection

## 13. SDL Questions

1. **What is the difference between outbreak, epidemic, and pandemic?** Scale and geography -- outbreak is localised, epidemic is regional, pandemic is global/international
2. **Who forms an OCT?** Consultant microbiologist, clinicians, infection control nurses in hospital setting
3. **What is NOIDS?** Notifications of Infectious Diseases -- mandatory reporting of specified diseases by registered medical practitioners
4. **Why is surveillance limited in LEDCs?** Lack of funding, limited diagnostic methods, fewer reference laboratories
5. **Why was the PHLS important?** Integrated network of 69 labs enabling flexible outbreak response and centralised data analysis at Colindale
